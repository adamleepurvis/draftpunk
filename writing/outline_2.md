THE KEDU
Book One
Full Outline

Theme
What you are willing to give, and why, and whether you are honest about the reason.

Four characters. Four different relationships to sacrifice:

•	Jalp: gave everything in the valley and spent eternity taking it back. The original betrayal of the theme.
•	Enki: gave incrementally, transactionally, for selfish reasons. The false protagonist. Lost himself in the accumulation of costs.
•	Nezha: gave the thing she always wanted, once, freely, for love. The purest version of the theme.
•	Lanu: gave the thing she was born with and never had to earn, to stop the man who started it all. Closes the circle Jalp opened.


Protagonist Note
This is Lanu's book. Nezha carries the emotional heart. Enki is the false protagonist whose betrayal of the theme is also a personal betrayal of Lanu.

Enki's opening misdirection is deliberate and structural. The reader needs to believe he is the protagonist long enough to feel the betrayal when he isn't. The query letter pitches Lanu. The book does the rest.


Characters
Lanu (Protagonist)
Kedu master-in-training in Tulo. Congenital gift -- she can hear the bashi without effort or training. Best friends with Nelo, the town bashi. Raised under Huli alongside Nezha.

Arc: Given everything freely. Has to learn what it costs to give something back.

Lanu arrives in Estopio grieving, closed off, and suspicious about Nelo's death. Finding Nelo's killer is her animating question in the city. Enki becomes the person she opens to -- the first person since Nelo who seems to see her clearly. He shows her the city, helps her get access she wouldn't otherwise have. The kiss is not a romantic subplot. It is the moment she is most vulnerable and most exposed.

In the valley, she names finding Nelo's killer as her price. The bashi deliver the answer: Huli. The truth is both what she wanted and what breaks her world again. She comes back having spent exactly what she offered, knowing everything, and having to face Huli, Enki's betrayal, and the confrontation alone -- with Hachi, without anyone she trusted at the start of the book.

The memory sacrifice at the end completes the arc. She paid to know the truth about Nelo's death. Then she gives up the memory of Nelo herself. She doesn't get to keep either -- the innocence before she knew, or the knowledge after. Just the facts, without the feeling.

"Until our hearts touch again" is her and Nelo's farewell phrase -- so ordinary it doesn't feel like a last word. It becomes the thread she reaches for throughout the book without knowing why it matters so much. After the memory sacrifice, the words survive. The meaning doesn't.

Misdirection note: Her arc requires at least one moment where she refuses or flinches before the sacrifice -- somewhere she has been quietly protecting that memory rather than being unaware it will matter.

Nezha (Co-Lead)
Kedu master, raised from orphancy by Huli. Devoted to him in a way that has been engineered from the beginning. Jealous of Lanu's congenital gift and her apparent carelessness with it. Meets Koya in the Estopio temple. Falls in love. Watches Huli kill Koya during the reveal. Asks the bashi to heal Koya. Gives up her newly acquired ability to hear them -- the thing she spent her life reaching for -- without hesitation. Koya lives. Nezha goes home to Tulo with Lanu and Koya, tending the tree without the ability she sacrificed.

Arc: Spent her life devoted to the wrong person, wanting the wrong thing. Gives up the right thing freely the moment she has it.

Nezha's jealousy is active friction in the plot, not just characterization. She is reluctant to go to Estopio, which reads as institutional loyalty to Huli but is driven underneath by jealousy of Lanu. She avoids Lanu in the city -- which is what creates the space for Koya. Her worst impulse accidentally gives her the best thing. She is quietly pleased when Lanu struggles; one specific beat in Estopio shows this clearly without stating it.

The key distinction: her jealousy is about the gift, not about Lanu personally. She wants what Lanu has. She doesn't wish Lanu harm. That distinction is what makes the sacrifice credible -- she gives up the ability without resentment, for love, the moment she has it.

Her ceremony price in the valley is TBD but was accepted by the tree, which means she was more honest with it than she has been with herself for most of her life.

Enki (False Protagonist)
Born into poverty in Estopio's Quag with the gift. Exploited by a system that conscripts the gifted into service of wealthy families. His sister had the gift, tried to connect to the Great Kedu without a bashi, lost herself -- alive but unreachable, housed in a Rings sick hall.

Arc: Legitimate grievance, wrong reasons, wrong guide. The tragedy of a man who was right about everything except what it justified.

Enki was contacted by Jalp's presence in the lian before the book begins -- through his sister. She is alive but unreachable in the sick hall, and when he sat with her trying to reach her, something reached back. Something that knew exactly what losing her felt like and exactly what he would do to get her back. He didn't find an ally. He found the thing that has been waiting for someone exactly like him.

By the time Enki meets Lanu he is already compromised. The warmth he feels toward her is real -- it is one of the last things running on fumes. His feelings may be genuine, as genuine as anything in him still is. But he never stops to ask whether he is helping her or being helped.

In the valley, the bashi don't pair with Enki. They sense he already has a guide. He comes back alone and frames it as independence. The voice in the lian already told him what it would mean.

He names an insufficient price in the valley -- something that sounds like sacrifice but isn't. The reservoir is shallow from the start and burns through almost immediately. The magic has been taking from him ever since. What's adjacent to what he named is his capacity for genuine connection -- his ability to see other people as ends rather than means. He loses it so gradually that each individual loss feels like a reasonable trade.

Degradation gradient: Three visible points of loss are needed in the draft. (1) Early: something small and social -- the charm blunted, a new bluntness people notice before he does. (2) Middle: something more essential -- ability to assess consequences, feel the weight of collateral damage. He kills the Rasa family leader here. The cause is just. He doesn't flinch the way he would have. (3) Late: the capacity to want his sister back in the way he wanted it. He has what he wanted and the wanting is gone.

Final image (TBD): The sick hall. His sister's hand. The voice that came through her. He remembers the origin of everything he became. He still can't say it wasn't worth it. Jalp's promise was hollow -- whether his sister recovers or remains lost is a single line in the denouement. The tragedy is that she may have been the instrument of everything he lost.

Misdirection note: Opening scene makes him feel like the protagonist. One planted detail -- he passed a woman struggling with a child on one hip and a basket twice her size on the other, thought briefly that he should help, and kept moving -- reads as relatable on first pass, damning on second. On first read: relatable. On second read: this is exactly who Enki is, and he was already gone before the book started.

Kaol (J'Rasa)
Born into one of Estopio's powerful Ring families. Has the gift. Has been sneaking below the Rings for years -- to the sick hall, to Eflo, to a baby named Yu. Approached by the Sakoku Society, who want him to plant a destructive seed in the valley during the ceremony. Refuses. His best friend Syca, already compromised by the Society, plants it instead -- quietly, almost accidentally. Kaol provides testimony that dismantles the Ring families, including Syca's. Syca disappears from his life without a confrontation. Kaol gives up his family's position. Stays in Estopio. Sets up a sick hall in the Rings. Eflo comes to help. Yu is there, older, learning to talk.

Arc: Privilege confronting itself and choosing to dismantle rather than reform. Loses the person he loved most because of a choice he made correctly.

His ceremony price in the valley is TBD but was genuine -- possibly naming something he didn't fully understand the consequences of, paying it cleanly throughout the book without flinching.

Nelo
Tulo's bashi. Vulpine. Chose Lanu -- when and why we never see. Has been watching Huli for a decade and can tell exactly two people: one is the source of the wrongness, one is a child she has been quietly preparing. Sends Lanu to the marsh the morning she dies. Doesn't look back. Her ears fold over in resignation. Everything she carried, alone, for ten years, becomes the thing Lanu eventually sacrifices.

The farewell scene between Lanu and Nelo is the most important scene in the book. The reader carries it forward through everything. "Until our hearts touch again" -- so ordinary it doesn't feel like a last word. What Nelo says, what she doesn't say, and what Lanu carries forward without knowing she is carrying it must be fully developed before drafting begins. The specific image of Nelo that the reader holds for the whole book -- the one that comes back when the memory sacrifice lands -- needs to be identified.

Huli / Jalp
Kedu master who arrived in Tulo a decade before the story begins. Raised Nezha from orphancy. Trained Lanu. Murdered the previous Tulo master to create the vacancy. Has been alive for an indeterminate number of generations by practicing kakari -- killing bashi to extend his life. The lian would detect him if he stayed in one place too long, so he keeps moving, engineering openings.

Discovered kakari the night before leading others into the valley -- understood what the bashi were, what killing them would give him, and fled before he could be detected. The Order of Jalp was built on his absence. He has been correcting the record ever since, in his own favor. He operates without a reservoir -- kakari keeps replacing what would otherwise be lost. He is not deteriorating because he keeps killing the thing that would let him deteriorate.

Misdirection note (prologue): The Order of Jalp was built on that proof -- and Jalp, it is said, never returned to correct it. Reads on first pass as a footnote about his mysterious disappearance. Reads on second pass as the first lie.

Koya
Temple leader in Estopio. Nezha's love. Killed by Huli during the reveal. Healed by the bashi at the cost of Nezha's ability to hear them.

Syca
Kaol's best friend. Already compromised by the Sakoku Society before the story begins. Plants the Sakoku seed in the valley when Kaol refuses -- a short, quiet scene, one image that sticks, the reader registers it without understanding it yet. Loses everything when Kaol dismantles the Ring families. Disappears without a confrontation. Book two territory.

His act is darker than it first appears. He didn't just fail when Kaol dismantled the families. He already did the thing he came to do.

Eflo
Bashi paired with the headmaster of Estopio's training school. Spends most of her time at the sick hall in the Spokes. Kaol's moral compass. Not interested in the institution's politics. The most useful person in the city.

Hachi
The bashi who pairs with Lanu in the valley. Stays with her after the memory sacrifice. A bashi choosing to remain with someone who can no longer remember why the bond exists is its own kind of answer about what the bond actually is.

At the close of the book, Hachi carries a tinge of notice -- something slightly wrong in the lian, below the threshold of what can be named. Lanu notices Hachi noticing but can't read it accurately anymore. The book ends with two characters who chose each other, sitting with something slightly wrong between them that neither can fully articulate.


Magic System
Junsi
The ability to send one's presence away from the body while remaining tethered to the self. Separating your presence from your body and merging into the consciousness of the kedu tree. The bashi guide you through the lian. Without the bashi to guide you back, connecting to the tree risks losing yourself in its overwhelming presence. The bashi is the difference between magic and madness.

The Bashi
The physical embodiment of the kedu tree's spirit. Vulpine creatures. Not animals -- they are the fruit of the kedu, revealed late in the book. Hearing the bashi is a congenital gift. The bashi is not just protection or guidance or emotional resonance. It is the safety mechanism of the entire system. It can feel the reservoir drawing down in real time during a session and must make the call to interrupt -- which the practitioner may resist and which Ring families actively work against.

The bashi bond also refills the reservoir. Abstention allows slow natural recovery. The bashi accelerates recovery significantly. Without a bashi, recovery is minimal. This creates a spectrum: strong bond recovers well, strained bond recovers poorly, no bashi essentially cannot recover.

The Material Economy
The kedu tree produces physical material -- sap, bark, leaves -- that is inert at best, mildly toxic at worst, without a practitioner to transmute it via junsi. The practitioner must be merged with the tree to transmute, but can do so in bulk -- one deep session activates a large quantity of material at once.

Transmuted material has medicinal properties, fertility effects on surrounding land, and luxury applications. Ground bark becomes a drug consumed by the wealthy -- a diluted echo of what junsi actually feels like, addictive for obvious reasons. A leaf or twig on a lapel is a status display. All of it required a practitioner to burn some of their reservoir to make it real.

Ring families use bulk transmutation to demand ever-larger batches per session. The bashi can feel the reservoir drawing down during a session in real time. The families create conditions where practitioners learn to override the warning -- through debt, through pressure, through managing the bond itself.

Coppicing
Deliberately stunting kedu trees to maximize harvestable material. Keeps the trees from reaching their full connection to the lian. The trees are being used against themselves. Coppicing also maximizes the transmutation work required from practitioners, which maximizes reservoir depletion, which maximizes how quickly practitioners burn out and need replacing. The Ring families have engineered a system that produces the most material in the shortest time at the highest human cost.

The Reservoir and Costs
In the valley, each practitioner names a price privately -- alone, in the swirling mists, with no witnesses. The named price becomes the fuel the magic burns when used. This is the reservoir. It is specific to what was offered, finite in ways the practitioner cannot fully measure, and invisible to everyone including themselves.

This is a one-time event. There is no returning to the valley to rename a price. The NLC is the single most consequential moment in any practitioner's life -- made alone, in mist, usually very young, without full understanding of what they are deciding.

The institution has a vested interest in keeping practitioners ignorant of the stakes. A practitioner who understood the naming was permanent and irreversible would name more carefully. The ritual and pageantry of the NLC is designed to convey weight without conveying information.

When the reservoir empties, the magic doesn't stop. It starts taking from what is adjacent to what was named. The specific shape of deterioration is personal -- it depends on what was offered and what surrounds it.

Degradation stages: 
•	Early: the edges of the named thing blur. Similar memories become less reliable, related emotions harder to access. Subtle enough to attribute to stress or grief.
•	Middle: the magic is clearly into something unnamed. Gaps appear. Judgment in specific areas becomes unreliable. The practitioner is still functional, still pursuing goals, but something is visibly wrong to people who knew them before.
•	Late: the magic takes broadly. Personality changes. The ability to assess consequences erodes. The self that named the price is no longer the self operating the body.
•	End stage: what happened to Enki's sister, compressed. What happens to Enki slowly.

Kakari
The practice of killing a bashi to extend one's life. Can be done multiple times to the same bashi, each time producing a rebirth without memory. Jalp discovered this and has been practicing it for generations.

Kakari bypasses the accumulation cost entirely. Jalp has been operating without a reservoir for generations -- the magic takes from him and kakari keeps replacing what is lost. He is not the tragedy of a man who burned through his price. He is a man who found a way to live outside the system indefinitely, at the cost of everything the bashi represent.

Kakari is also the removal of the only safety mechanism. Jalp doesn't just kill creatures -- he removes the thing that would tell him when to stop, the thing that would refill what the magic takes, and the thing that makes the magic survivable for anyone who bonds with him.

The Lian
The connectedness of the bashi network. The trees communicate through it. Practitioners navigate it via junsi. Jalp's presence in the lian is how he finds Enki. It is also what turns against him at the end.

The lian is neutral infrastructure with something adjacent to agency -- not fully conscious but not entirely passive either. Its relationship to Jalp (corrupted presence), to Enki (claimed guide), and to Lanu (turned against Jalp at the end) needs to feel consistent and mysterious rather than mechanically explained.

The Ceremony Price -- What We Know Per Character
Lanu: Names finding Nelo's killer. The bashi deliver the answer in the valley: Huli. The truth is what she wanted and what breaks her world again. She comes back having spent exactly what she offered.

Enki: Names something insufficient -- framed as sacrifice, actually convenient. The reservoir is shallow from the start. The magic burns through it almost immediately and has been taking from him ever since. He names it privately and the reader never learns exactly what it was, only what it cost.

Kaol: Names something genuine, probably without fully understanding the consequences. TBD -- his price likely anticipates the loss of Syca or his family position without his knowing it.

Nezha: TBD. Whatever she named was accepted, which means she was more honest with the tree than she has been with herself for most of her life.


World
Tulo
Small keechad town along the Stone Path. Home to Lanu, Nezha, Huli, Nelo. The kedu tree in the central courtyard. The keechad marshes surrounding it. Where the book begins and ends. A practitioner who tends rather than a practitioner who is consumed. Sustainable, not profitable.

The Keechad
The marshlands. The pool of Fero and Naru's tears, according to legend. Lanu's home ground. Her practice in the keechad is what makes junsi come naturally to her in the valley -- the marsh trained her to move through something overwhelming without losing herself.

Estopio
The capital. Built around the Great Kedu tree. Divided into the Rings (wealthy families), the Spokes (middle class), the Hub (center), and the Quag (the poor, built on ground too soggy for stone). The Quag has no plumbing -- the river running through it is not just water. The bridges across it are named the Father and the Mother, after the mountains watching over the city.

The Rings
Wealthy families who control the kedu trade. The J'Rasa family is among them. The coppicing of kedu trees -- deliberately stunted to produce more harvestable material -- keeps the trees from reaching their full connection to the lian. The trees are being used against themselves. The Rings are more self-sufficient and fertile because of their proximity to transmuted kedu output. A leaf or twig on a lapel is a status display.

The Quag
Produces the gifted. The Rings consume them. The practitioner can't explain what is being taken from them because the price was private. There is no evidence, no recourse. Just a person who went into the valley whole and is coming apart by increments in service of a Ring family's interests.

The Order of Jalp
The religious institution built on Jalp's founding myth. Has been captured by the Ring families from the inside. The institution controls access to bashi pairing at the NLC -- a practitioner paired with a strong assertive bashi is harder to exploit, so the institution has been quietly engineering weaker pairings for generations without anyone naming that is what is happening.

The Jalp Valley / Valley of the Mother and Father
Where the Great Kedu grows. Site of the New Light Ceremony. Where Jalp first went alone and came back changed. Where the naming of prices happens -- privately, in swirling mist, with no witnesses.

The New Light Ceremony (NLC)
Annual rite of passage for new magic practitioners. Candidates enter the valley, name their price privately in the mist, and may be paired with a bashi. Has become the mechanism by which the institution controls access to magic. The bashi do not pair with everyone -- Enki comes back alone, and the reader eventually understands why.

The Stone Path
The main route connecting Tulo, Maka, and Estopio.

The Sakoku Society
A secretive group dedicated to destroying the Great Kedu and restoring the old ways -- stone over keechad, the mountains' spirits returned. They have the core of Utga, the mountain whose tantrum froze the world.

Their mechanism in book one: the seed Syca plants in the valley poisons the ground and interrupts the lian -- making the kedu trees uninhabitable over time. The effect is medium-slow. By the close of book one, the seed is in the ground and nobody knows it yet except Hachi, who carries a tinge of notice below the threshold of what can be named.

Book two will deal with the impact. Some damage will be irretrievable. The lian can survive if they go to the valley of the Great Kedu to address it directly -- which means Lanu must return to the place where she made the memory sacrifice.

Structural Notes
The prologue should be short -- five sentences of received history, written in the register of official record. The Jalp prologue plants the flag that pays off at the end.

The legend of Fero and Naru belongs inside the narrative. A character recites it, or it appears carved somewhere, or it is told at the New Light Ceremony. Embedded mythology lands harder than prefaced mythology.

The historical interludes need a consistent voice -- possibly the format of official record, decree, or institutional history, which reinforces that history in this world is official and therefore unreliable.

Lanu and Nelo's farewell is the most important scene in the book. The reader carries it forward through everything. When the memory sacrifice lands at the end, that morning is what they flash back to. It needs to be perfect.

Enki's opening establishes the misdirection. He is charming, funny, resourceful, sympathetic -- and entirely self-oriented, already compromised, the reservoir already gone. The planted sentence does the work quietly. On first read: relatable. On second read: this is exactly who Enki is, and has been for longer than the book shows.

The tinge of wrongness in the lian at the close -- felt by Hachi, noticed by Lanu without being understood -- is the bridge to book two. The victory is real. The catastrophe is already in the ground.
